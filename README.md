## The situation, plainly

The November 2023 injury turned out to be the visible edge of something bigger. The imaging shows six degenerative cervical discs — most people my age have one or two — a few severe, with anomalies that may or may not stay put. This spring, during my second round of PT, an entire autonomic symptom track surfaced: neurological symptoms that don't correlate cleanly with the pain track and that periodically take days at a time from me. The clinical picture at the end of that round: a structurally compromised spine I can't do much about, and a depleted nervous system I can.

That second part is where this project lives.

## What the project actually is

One daily practice, two outputs.

**Output one: the book.** *A Somatic Unraveling: Field Notes from the Cremation Grounds* is a medical narrative — a genre with real scholarly grounding (Arthur Frank's *The Wounded Storyteller* is the anchor text) that treats the patient's account of illness as serious knowledge, not just testimony. The book asks a specific question: what happens to a person's identity when the body that defined it — athlete, bartender, documentarian hauling gear through post-conflict situations, commercial producer — stops being able to produce? Not a recovery memoir, because recovery isn't the honest frame. A record of recalibration: what gets rebuilt, how, and what it costs.

I'm three months into a daily morning writing practice that generates the raw material (as of July 2026). The writing happens with zero AI involvement — that boundary is absolute and part of the project's design. A published excerpt shows what the finished register looks like: [Body as Altar, Neck as Oracle](https://signalsandseeds.xyz/body-as-altar-neck-as-oracle/)

**Output two: the data.** During this spring's PT round, I built what I call a [HealingOS](https://www.commsos.org/what-is-a-healingos/) — a structured daily tracking system logging both symptom tracks with consistent metrics, producing short printed summaries for my providers covering what happened between sessions. It solved a real clinical problem: my therapists were working from a generic "general neck pain" classification and my in-session recall, which is noisy and non-clinical. The tracking data let us see that the two symptom tracks don't move together, which redirected the treatment. That's not a hypothetical benefit — it already happened once.

> The larger point: patients with complex chronic conditions are usually the worst-documented part of their own care. The system sees them seven minutes at a time. The HealingOS makes the patient the best-documented part instead. Eventually the methodology gets published openly so other patients can build their own.

## The experimental phase — what I'm doing for the next 12–24 months

A structured, documented recalibration program. The premise: the spine is what it is, but nervous system function can be rebuilt, and I'm unusually well-positioned to run this experiment on myself. My Yoga Studies Bachelors degree included formal training in nervous system function and trauma physiology — I was trained to do this work for others and am now applying it to my own case.

The program combines:

- **Traditional track:** orthopedic oversight, physical therapy (a third round, eventually private PT for more individualized work)
- **Holistic track:** yoga therapy, cranial sacral work, and my own contemplative and somatic practices
- **Documentation:** everything logged through the HealingOS with the same metrics, so the modalities can actually be compared over time

This is where the experiment either earns the word or doesn't: consistent daily data across modalities, over a long enough window to see patterns, with clinical eyes on it.

## What I'm asking — specific roles

**The clinical collaboration:**

This experiment runs with clinical eyes on it, not around them. The orthopedic surgeon who has held this case from the beginning — the original imaging, the MRI history, the surgical consult — is being invited into the project in a defined set of roles:

- **The imaging arc.** The book and the dataset should rest on the actual clinical record, not the patient's memory of appointments. That means access to the full imaging history, interpreted with the physician who ordered it.
- **Clinical annotation.** Periodic review of the HealingOS data with a clinician's eye: is what's being captured meaningful, what's missing, and what would make patient-generated data useful to a doctor rather than just to the patient.
- **Safety oversight.** The experimental program has a clinical backstop — a physician who knows the specific anomalies in this spine and can flag when an approach is unwise. Self-experimentation without that backstop is recklessness; with it, it's documented case work.
- **Possibly more, later.** Patient-generated data as a clinical tool is a live conversation in medicine right now, and this project is a documented working example. If the data side matures the way it's designed to, a deeper collaboration between patient and physician may be worth pursuing. That door stays open without being forced.

**For close friends:**

- **The honest version of the timeline:** 12–24 months of experiments and daily writing, then a year of manuscript work, likely somewhere quieter than Longmont. This project is also my transition plan out of this chapter of life. That's a feature.
- **What support actually looks like:** check-ins that don't require me to perform being okay. Patience when plans collapse — the neurological days take what they take. Honest reads when I share drafts or excerpts. And when the public pieces launch (a field-notes site and a subscription later this year), sharing them with anyone you know navigating their own chronic situation.
- **What I don't need:** solutions, supplements, or the name of someone's miracle practitioner — unless I ask, and sometimes I will.
- **The single-person reality:** I'm running this solo, without a safety net, which is exactly why the infrastructure exists. But infrastructure doesn't replace people. The check-ins matter more than they look like they do.

## Why this matters beyond me

Millions of people carry chronic, invisible, destabilizing conditions through a medical system that sees them in seven-minute fragments. The literature about that experience mostly documents what the system does to a body. This project documents what a patient built back — the narrative AND the tools — and hands both over. That's the whole point: someone in their own freefall finds this, and it helps them find the floor.
